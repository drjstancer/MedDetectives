# The Jordan Carter Case

## Full Narrative Scenario Framework

### MedDetectives Collaborative Clinical Reasoning Experience

---

# Core Educational Purpose

This scenario is designed to help undergraduate pre-med students practice:

- collaborative reasoning
- evidence synthesis
- uncertainty tolerance
- communication
- observational interpretation
- reflective meaning-making
- escalation awareness
- emotional intelligence
- team-based decision-making

This is NOT a murder mystery.

This IS an evolving wellness and behavioral escalation investigation.

The educational tension comes from incomplete information and conflicting interpretations.

---

# Narrative Premise

Jordan Carter is a second-year university student involved in a highly competitive interdisciplinary student leadership organization.

Jordan recently participated in a major campus showcase event where multiple students, faculty members, and peers observed changes in behavior, communication, emotional regulation, and participation patterns.

Several days later, Jordan experienced a significant emotional and physiological escalation event during a late-night collaborative planning session.

The MedDetectives team has been asked to reconstruct the timeline, analyze conflicting observations, evaluate evolving evidence, and determine what warning signs may have been missed.

Participants are NOT diagnosing Jordan.

Participants are instead trying to determine:

- what happened,
- when changes emerged,
- why interpretations differed,
- and how collaborative reasoning can improve support-oriented decision making.

---

# Jordan Carter — Character Profile

Age: 20
Year: Sophomore
Major: Biological Sciences
Leadership Role: Student Event Coordinator

Jordan is widely perceived as:

- dependable
- calm
- high-achieving
- socially engaged
- academically driven

Jordan has:

- multiple leadership obligations
- demanding coursework
- family pressure
- inconsistent sleep patterns
- increasing social withdrawal

Very few people noticed the gradual accumulation of stress indicators.

---

# Stage 1 — Orientation

Participants receive:

- case briefing
- initial witness overview
- evidence board
- partial timeline

Initial understanding:
Jordan experienced a sudden stress-related escalation event.

The group initially assumes the event appeared unexpectedly.

---

# Stage 2 — Initial Discovery

## QR Discovery #1 — “Shift in Emotional Presentation”

Participants discover Jordan had shown subtle emotional withdrawal weeks before the escalation event.

Examples include:

- delayed responses
- reduced participation
- unfinished messages
- fatigue indicators
- social disengagement

This discovery introduces timeline uncertainty.

---

# Witness Statements

## Witness A — Team President

“Jordan always seemed composed to me. Maybe tired sometimes, but nothing alarming.”

## Witness B — Close Friend

“Jordan stopped joking around like usual. Something felt different.”

## Witness C — Faculty Advisor

“I noticed increased perfectionism and frustration during planning meetings.”

Participants must determine why different people interpreted the same behaviors differently.

---

# Stage 3 — Timeline Conflict

Participants receive fragmented timeline cards.

Contradictions emerge:

- Jordan missed one meeting unexpectedly
- a journal reflection references exhaustion
- text timestamps conflict with witness memories
- a planning document shows Jordan working at 3:12 AM

The group begins debating whether warning signs were overlooked.

---

# Stage 4 — Hidden Reflection Fragment

## QR Discovery #2

Participants uncover a partially hidden journal fragment:

“I feel like everyone only notices me when I’m useful.”

The group must now consider:

- emotional masking
- social pressure
- internalized expectations
- performative wellness

---

# Stage 5 — Escalation Evidence

## PIN Unlock Discovery

Participants unlock:

- contradictory observations
- fragmented communication attempts
- incomplete outreach messages
- evidence of sleep disruption
- planning overload

One key discovery:
Jordan attempted to ask for help indirectly, but the signals were interpreted differently by different people.

---

# Contradictory Evidence Layer

Participants discover that confidence does not equal accuracy.

One highly confident witness misremembered the sequence of events entirely.

Another uncertain witness provided the most accurate observations.

This becomes the central reasoning lesson.

---

# Stage 6 — Collaborative Synthesis

The team must collaboratively answer:

- What evidence became most significant?
- Which assumptions changed over time?
- What indicators were overlooked?
- Why did interpretations differ?
- What role did uncertainty play?
- How could collaborative support systems improve outcomes?

There is no single “correct answer.”

Reasoning quality determines success.

---

# Intended Emotional Arc

Participants should move through:

certainty → curiosity → contradiction → uncertainty → reflection → collaborative synthesis

---

# Facilitator Guidance

The facilitator should:

- avoid giving direct answers
- redirect assumptions
- encourage evidence-based discussion
- reinforce collaborative interpretation
- normalize uncertainty

Facilitator prompts:

- “What evidence supports that interpretation?”
- “What assumptions might the group be making?”
- “Could two conflicting observations both contain truth?”

---

# Final Educational Message

People rarely experience challenges in ways that are fully visible to others.

Collaborative reasoning requires humility, reflection, communication, and a willingness to revisit assumptions when new evidence emerges.
